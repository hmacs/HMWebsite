Email Security Best Practices for 2021

Keep your identity and data safe with these email safety tips and tricks.

Top email security best practices for employees

Follow these guidelines to keep your data safe!

  • Take Precaution!

    • This is mentioned multiple different ways on our tips below, but is so important that it has to be top of our list. Take precaution at all times. If you are walking down a sidewalk, would you give your SSN or credit card details to someone just because they ask you for it? Even some of the most complicated phishing and ransomware attacks can happen because of a simple mistake. Be cautious, and if anything sounds out of the ordinary, be sure to take extra steps to double-check if the email is real.

  • Think before you click

    • Never download or open attachments and links from unknown senders. Even when the senders name looks correct, you should take precautions. If you were not expecting the attachment, double-check that the email is real. Hover your mouse over the senders name and make sure the email address is correct. Hover the mouse over any links and look over the web address. If a company sends you an email asking you to correct an account issue and has a “Log In” button, avoid clicking the button and go directly to their webpage to sign into your account.

  • Look for Phishing Scams

    • Phishing is an attempt to trick you into thinking the email came from a real person or company. Look for grammatical errors, inconsistencies in the email, and as mentioned before, hover over any link without clicking it to verify what the link is. When in doubt, ask someone in IT.

  • Leave your personal information personal.

    • As tempting as filling out the social media surveys may be, it exposes information about you that hackers can and do use. School you graduated, city you grew up in, first pet, childhood nickname… these are some of the most used security questions to gain access to accounts. When you answer these, you leave yourself exposed to hackers that would otherwise know nothing about you.

  • Update your devices, or have a professional update them for you.

    • Updates don’t just add cool new features, they almost always deliver security updates and patches as well. If your phone or computer is behind in updates, it will be more vulnerable to security issues.

  • Be Proactive

    • Despite what you might have heard, Apple products are no longer immune to malware or ransomware. Have your PC or Mac protected with antivirus. The Heartland Macs monitored antivirus is Enterprise level software, but priced to be ready for both homes and office. In the end, just be sure you have something reliable protecting your computer.

  • Back up your data

    • Your computer shouldn’t be the only location your data is stored and cloud backups are as cheap as $10 a month (including Heartland Macs monitored backup system). When ransomware happens, all of your files are lost and the only option is to recover from backups.

  • Know that you are a target

    • To hackers, small businesses and employees of small businesses are their #1 targets. 43% of cyber attacks are targeting small businesses. 60% of small businesses will fail within 6 months of falling to a cyber-attack with no protection in place. Hackers can gain the most money by attacking this, more often than not, vulnerable market.

  • Connect Securely

    • Every tech under the sun has said this at least once… Don’t connect to a wide open wireless network. Password protected wireless networks are not just to keep people off of the network. That security key encrypts your wireless data between your computer and the wireless router. When you are on a wireless network that is open, your data is being sent unsecured and anyone even in the parking lot could capture it.

  • Password protect your devices

    • For best security, use a password manager that will generate and securely store secure passwords. But password protect your phone, laptop and use different passwords for websites.
